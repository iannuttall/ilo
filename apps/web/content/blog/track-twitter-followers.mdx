You can track Twitter followers over time in four realistic ways: native X analytics, a spreadsheet, a public tracker, or a tool like ilo. The awkward part is history. Most tools can only chart the data they have already collected.

If you did not record your follower count six months ago, you usually cannot reconstruct a perfect daily chart today. That is the bit people find out after they need the chart. Classic.

## Native X analytics is fine for a quick pulse

[X Analytics includes account-level reporting](https://business.x.com/en/advertising/analytics), post activity, follower and impression recaps, and CSV export for post activity. It is the place to start if you only need to answer simple questions about your own account.

Use native X when you want to check whether your follower count is moving, see which posts are doing well, or export recent post data. It has the big advantage of being first-party. You are not asking another service to guess.

The limitation is that native analytics is built for checking performance, not maintaining a long independent history. If you want a clean follower chart that you can compare month after month, you need to keep your own record somewhere else.

## A spreadsheet gives you control but no forgiveness

The simplest tracker is still a spreadsheet. Pick a schedule, copy your follower count, and chart the total plus the daily or weekly change.

That setup works surprisingly well for small accounts.

- It costs nothing.
- You decide the exact cadence.
- You can add notes for launches, viral posts, campaigns, or account changes.
- You can calculate growth rate however you like.

The trade-off is obvious. If you forget to log the count, the gap stays there. If you mistype a number, the chart lies. If you want to track five accounts, the whole thing turns into a little admin job you will resent by week three.

## Public trackers are useful when they already have the account

Public follower trackers can be useful for looking up another account, benchmarking a competitor, or checking whether a big account has obvious spikes and drops. They are weaker for personal record-keeping because they depend on what the tracker has already captured.

This is the core rule. A public tracker can show its own history for an account. It cannot magically recover private daily snapshots that it never collected.

That makes public trackers best for rough comparison, not audit-grade history. They can also break when X changes access, rate limits, or page structure. If a tracker has no record for the account you care about, you are back to starting today.

## ilo starts history when you connect the account

ilo tracks your connected account counters, including X followers, and keeps the history from the day the account is connected. It is better than a spreadsheet when you want the numbers collected for you, and better than a random public tracker when the account is yours and accuracy matters.

It still cannot recover data from before the connection. No honest tool can promise that unless it already had a historical dataset for the account. What ilo gives you is a clean daily record from this point forward, shown alongside your other counters so follower growth is not floating in a vacuum.

The hosted ilo tracker has been retired. Use the steps above, or fork the [open source ilo repository](https://github.com/iannuttall/ilo) if you want to build local follower-history imports.

## Pick the method based on the question

Use native X analytics when you want a quick first-party view of your own account. Use a spreadsheet when you want a free, manual log and do not mind the routine. Use a public tracker when you are looking at someone else's account and rough history is enough.

Use ilo when you want your own follower history collected automatically from now on, especially if you care about seeing followers beside posts, streaks, and other account counters.

The practical answer is boring but reliable. Start tracking before you need the chart.
