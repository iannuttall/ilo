Twitter Spaces analytics used to sound like a tidy dashboard problem. Today it is messier. X still supports Spaces, but the useful host view is thin, API access is lifecycle-limited, and the best hosts measure the parts they can actually act on.

## X still supports Spaces, but analytics are limited

X's [Spaces help page](https://help.x.com/en/using-x/spaces) says anyone can join, listen, and speak in a Space on iOS and Android, while web users can currently listen. It also says Spaces are public, can be shared with a link, and can have up to 13 speakers at the same time including the host and co-hosts.

For measurement the most concrete in-product number X documents is attendance after a recorded Space ends. X says that when a recorded Space ends, the host sees how many people attended and gets a link to share the replay.

That is useful, but it is thin. It does not replace a proper show dashboard with retention, speaker share, audience source, replay drop-off, and topic history. If you are running Spaces seriously, you need a lightweight scorecard of your own.

## Attendance tells you whether the topic had pull

Attendance is the first metric because it is the easiest to compare. It answers whether the topic, host, guest, timing, and promotion were strong enough to get people into the room.

Do not overread it. A small Space with the right 30 people can beat a big room full of distracted listeners. Still, attendance gives you a baseline, especially when you compare similar formats against each other.

Track it simply.

- Scheduled topic.
- Host and guest.
- Start time.
- Peak visible listener count if you captured it live.
- Final attendance if the Space was recorded.
- Replay link.

The best time to record this is immediately after the Space ends. Waiting until tomorrow is how the numbers disappear into vibes.

## Participation shows whether the room became a conversation

Participation is the second metric because Spaces are live audio, and live audio gets awkward when nobody joins in. X's docs say listeners can request permission to speak, and hosts can add speakers or co-hosts during a Space.

For community-led rooms, track how many people spoke and how concentrated the speaking time was. You do not need perfect transcription to learn something. A simple note like "five speakers, two dominated" is enough to improve the next session.

For interview-style rooms, participation means something else. You might want fewer speakers, cleaner Q&A, and better questions rather than more voices. That is why the metric needs to match the format. A town hall and a founder interview should not be graded with the same ruler.

## Replay and follow-up show whether the Space had a second life

Recording matters because it gives the Space another chance to work. X's [hosting guide](https://help.x.com/en/using-x/spaces-hosting) says recorded Spaces become publicly available for replay after the live session, and hosts can share the recording by post.

Measure the follow-up assets, not only the room.

- Did the replay post get replies, reposts, bookmarks, or profile clicks?
- Did clips or quotes from the conversation outperform the original promo?
- Did the guest share it?
- Did the Space lead to newsletter replies, DMs, trials, or calls?

That last set will not come from X automatically. It is still worth tracking because most business value from a good Space happens after the room closes.

## API data helps while the Space is live or scheduled

The [X API Spaces docs](https://docs.x.com/x-api/spaces/introduction) say Spaces endpoints can look up live or scheduled Spaces, search Spaces, and expose roles such as hosts, speakers, and listener counts through participant data. They also say Spaces have a lifecycle and become unavailable through those endpoints once ended.

That lifecycle matters. If you want API-based Twitter Spaces analytics, collect what you need while the Space is live or scheduled. After the Space ends, you may be left with your recording, your posts, and whatever you captured yourself.

For most hosts a small manual scorecard is enough. For teams build a capture process around the live window. Either way, the goal is the same. Learn which topics bring the right people into the room, which formats produce useful participation, and which follow-ups turn the conversation into something durable.

If your account-level metrics are scattered across X analytics, your inbox, and a spreadsheet, give that evidence to your agent with a clear date range and sample. The [agent brief report](/reports/agent-brief) helps keep the Spaces follow-up next to the audience and engagement trends that matter.
